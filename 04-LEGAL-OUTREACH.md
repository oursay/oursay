## Questions Identified from Platform Documentation

*These are the legal questions that should be answered before the platform launches. They are
woven into both email versions below, but are listed here for reference when working with legal
counsel.*

**1. Platform Classification**
The platform is currently described as "civic infrastructure" and a "civic platform." Does this framing create legal risk — implied quasi-governmental status, proximity to election law, or unintended regulatory classification? Would registration as a social media platform centered on political discussion be legally cleaner, with verification offered as a paid feature? What classification minimizes regulatory exposure while preserving the platform's mission?

**2. Implied Government or Electoral Approval**
A government non-affiliation disclaimer has been added to all public-facing documents. Does the remaining language — referencing identity and residency verification, district assignments, and a proposed Elections Alberta partnership — still read as implying official provincial approval or quasi-official status? Are the disclaimers as written legally sufficient, or is stronger language or placement required?

**3. The Word "Public Vote"**
The platform has renamed its formal vote feature from "Referendum" to "Public Vote" to avoid direct conflict with Alberta's *Referendum Act*. Does "Public Vote" create any remaining legal risk, confusion with official processes, or trigger any obligations? Are there any other content feature names (petition, belief) that warrant legal review?

**4. Third-Party Advertiser Status During Elections**
During a federal or provincial election writ period, does the platform's activity — hosting political petitions, referendums, and belief voting — trigger third-party advertiser registration or spending limit obligations under the *Canada Elections Act* or Alberta's *Election Act*? Does this apply to the platform itself, to users posting content, or both? Does it matter whether OurSay charges for any service during that period?

**5. Identity and Residency Verification Claims**
The platform has replaced "elector eligibility determination" language with "identity and residency verification." Verified users are described as confirmed real persons living at a verified address — no claim is made about electoral eligibility or voter registration status. Does this approach adequately avoid encroaching on Elections Alberta's or Elections Canada's jurisdiction? Is there any remaining language or implication the platform should address?

**6. The "Refused to Verify" Public Status**
The platform permanently records on a user's public profile that they received a sponsored KYC verification and did not complete it within 30 days. This status cannot be removed. Does this create defamation risk — particularly if the refusal was caused by a KYC system failure, a dispute with the provider, or circumstances beyond the user's control? What conditions, disclosures, or appeal mechanisms would reduce this risk?

**7. Public Official Profiles**
The platform creates profiles for MLAs, MPs, and municipal officials based on public record, without requiring their consent. Their name, official role, district, and party affiliation are displayed. Are there consent, misrepresentation, or defamation concerns with this approach? What happens if an official objects?

**8. Corporate Structure**
What entity type is legally optimal for this platform — provincial corporation, federal corporation, non-profit society, cooperative? How does the choice of structure affect: liability exposure, applicability of election law, ability to receive donations or sponsorship funding, and public perception of neutrality?

**9. KYC Data Obligations**
The platform processes identity verification data through a third-party provider to confirm identity, age, and address. What are OurSay's obligations as a data processor under Alberta's *Personal Information Protection Act* (PIPA) and federal *PIPEDA*? Does the civic participation use case create any obligations beyond standard identity verification?

**10. Canadian Data Residency**
The platform proposes to host on Google Cloud or AWS, with servers potentially outside Canada. Are there data residency requirements that apply to this platform given that it processes Canadian identity and elector data? Does the answer change if the platform is incorporated provincially vs. federally?

**11. Intermediary Liability for User Content**
Canada does not have a direct equivalent to the U.S.'s Section 230. What is OurSay's liability exposure for user-generated petitions, beliefs, and discussion comments that may be defamatory, misleading, or otherwise legally problematic? What moderation obligations exist?

**12. The Sponsorship Payment Model**
Any user can pay for another user's identity verification. The platform processes this payment as a pass-through. Does this constitute a regulated payment or financial service? Does it create any contractual or fiduciary relationship between the sponsor, the platform, and the recipient?

**13. Liability for Published Results**
The public documents describe referendum results as "immutable" and "permanent." If a result is later found to be compromised, manipulated, or incorrect, does this language create heightened liability for the platform? What disclaimers are needed?

**14. Online Harms Act Exposure**
If Canada's Online Harms Act (or successor legislation) comes into force, would OurSay fall within its scope? What obligations would that create, and are there structural decisions the platform should make now to reduce future compliance burden?

**15. KYC Provider Terms of Use**
Does the chosen KYC provider's (initially Equifax Connect) service agreement permit using their verification output in the way OurSay intends — specifically: publishing an identity and residency confirmation as a public-facing verification tier badge, assigning geographic district data, and using the result as the basis for a verification status displayed on a user profile?
